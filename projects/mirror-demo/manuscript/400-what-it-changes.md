She went back to work on Monday. She did not file a fidelity objection. She did not flag her own reconstruction the way she had flagged the surgeon's.

David asked how her weekend was. She said fine.

In the verification lab she opened a new case: a parole board in Pennsylvania, three decisions from the previous quarter. The Mirror's reconstructions were clean and narrow. She verified each one in four hours and signed off without hesitation.

At the end of the day Soo-Jin stopped by the lab. "I read the internal reports. The board is pleased. They want to publish a transparency brief."

"Good," Vera said.

"Your file was interesting."

Vera looked at her.

"I'm not going to ask about it," Soo-Jin said. "I just want to know if you think the system got it right."

"The system doesn't get things right or wrong. It maps causes."

"That's not what I asked."

Vera thought about the surgeon at Methodist, who would have said the daughter's legal career never crossed his mind. She thought about herself reading thirty-two pages on her bed in Somerville, recognizing every link in the chain and still not recognizing the person it described.

"It got it right," she said. "I'm just not sure that changes anything."

Soo-Jin nodded as if that was the answer she expected, which, Vera supposed, it was.
